# THE HUNDRED — 100 Days to the WSOP Main Event

*One third gladiator (Foxen) · one third Mad Max (Deeb) · one third zen monk (Mateos)*

The WSOP Main Event is a $10K freezeout marathon — 60,000-chip stacks, 120-minute levels, four starting flights, money bubble early on Day 4. This program prepares you for it in five phases of twenty days, roughly 2.5–4.5 hours per day (scale down 40% or up 50% without breaking the sequence). Every 7th day consolidates; every 20th is an exam.

## The Doctrine: aggressive, but survivalist

| Dimension | Gladiator (Foxen) | Mad Max (Deeb) | Zen Monk (Mateos) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Baseline | GTO for foundation, exploit for profit — "understand, don't memorize" | Exploit-driven; every bet must tell a believable story | No fixed strategy — aggressive or tight by stage and opponents |
| Signature weapon | Fearless high-leverage preflop escalation (the $25K six-bet jam) | Credible over-representation of nutted ranges; relentless grind | Theory-approved bluff-catches under maximum pressure |
| Steal this | Baseline-first understanding and fearless leverage | Grind discipline and believable aggression | The adaptive pressure throttle |

## The War Map: survival vs aggression by tournament day

| Phase | Ratio (survival/aggression) | Mode | In practice |
|---|---|---|---|
| Day 1 flights | 60/40 | Exploit & observe | Tag every player first orbit; iso weak limpers bigger; value-bet recs hard; no ego wars with regs at 250bb |
| Day 2 | 55/45 | Selective accumulation | Keep exploiting weak opens; trim thin stack-offs vs solid players; protect stack utility |
| Day 3 | 50/50 | Pressure with discipline | More LP steals, fewer autopilot OOP flats, enter every hand with a turn-and-river plan |
| Day 4 bubble | 75/25 covered · 35/65 covering | Asymmetric ICM war | Covered: cut marginal opens, low-variance lines. Covering: relentless steals, blocker-heavy 3-bets |
| Day 5 | 45/55 | Re-accelerate | Attack post-cash pay-jump psychology; punish under-defending medium stacks |
| Day 6 | 50/50 | Precision over volume | Efficient steals, no heroics, simplify short-stack decisions |
| Days 7–8 | 65/35 mid · 40/60 big | ICM & leverage | Middling stacks avoid collisions; leaders punish capped ranges and risk premiums |
| Final table | Role-based | ICM warfare | Leader pressures; middle avoids symmetric disasters; short waits for profitable jams, never blinds out |

## Daily non-negotiables (the vows)

- **Sleep** — 7–9 hours, every night
- **Movement** — 20–30 min walking/mobility most days; 2 strength sessions weekly
- **Water** — at wake-up, between study blocks, at every break
- **Breath** — 5–10 minutes breathing, journaling, or visualization
- **Process** — mark hands, review hands, write one lesson learned, daily

# Trial of the Three Thirds (quiz answer key)

Take it cold on Day 1, again on Day 50, again on Day 90. Six questions per third; your lowest-scoring third gets the extra drilling.

## ⚔️ The Blade (Gladiator)

**Q1. Day 1, 250 big blinds deep. A weak recreational player limps in middle position and you hold A♠9♠ on the button. Your default?**

- Limp behind and keep the pot small
- **✓ Iso-raise, and size it bigger than your standard raise**
- Fold — A9s is dominated territory
- Min-raise to keep his whole range in

> Isoing weak limpers bigger is the Day 1 bread and butter: you buy position, initiative, and a dominated calling range from the player type that pays off the whole field.

**Q2. You c-bet the flop and the turn is a total brick. Which hands keep barreling?**

- Every draw — keep the pressure on
- Only your value hands; give up all bluffs
- **✓ Value hands plus the best blocker-rich semibluffs**
- Whatever your gut says — the brick changes nothing

> When the turn bricks, continue with value plus the best semibluffs and blocker-rich gutshots — not every draw in sight. Turn barreling is selective violence.

**Q3. Compared to flop betting, your turn betting range should generally become…**

- Wider and smaller — keep everyone in
- **✓ More polarized and more river-aware**
- More merged — bet all your medium hands
- Identical; consistency is key

> By the turn, strategy polarizes: strong value and robust bluffs continue while medium-strength hands check more. Turn sizing is about pressure and the river SPR you're creating.

**Q4. The lesson from Foxen's six-bet jam with 18 left in a WSOP $25K is…**

- Six-bet jam more often — aggression always wins
- Never play big preflop pots without the nuts
- **✓ Study rare high-leverage trees, because when one arises it's for your tournament life**
- Only elite pros should 4-bet bluff

> The right prep question isn't 'how often will this happen?' but 'am I ready if it happens once for my tournament life?' Deep leverage nodes are the highest-EV spots most players never study.

**Q5. Before you raise the turn as a bluff, Deeb's doctrine demands you answer: what am I repping, why can I have it, why can't villain — and…?**

- How much of my stack does it risk?
- **✓ Which opponent type actually folds?**
- What are the pot odds?
- Will the table respect me after?

> Bluffs must tell a believable story to an opponent capable of folding. If the answer to 'who folds?' is an unknown calling station — save the chips.

**Q6. You face a big river bet. The best bluff-catchers are hands that…**

- Block villain's value and block his bluffs
- **✓ Beat his bluff region and don't heavily block his natural bluffs**
- Have the best absolute strength, full stop
- Beat at least half his value range

> The Mateos model: his A♦2♦ call was right because it beat the bluffs and didn't block them — profitable against the range even though it lost the actual hand. Call because the range says so.

## 🏜️ The Wasteland (Mad Max)

**Q7. The Main Event is a freezeout — no re-entry. Versus a re-entry event, your stack-off thresholds should be…**

- Identical — chips are chips
- Looser: you must accumulate while you can
- **✓ Tighter: tournament life carries extra value all event long**
- Tighter only at the final table

> No second rig in the wasteland. In a freezeout, preserve tournament life more carefully — ICM pressure compounds from Day 1, and the survival premium never switches off.

**Q8. Day 4 money bubble. Every stack at your table covers you. Your mode?**

- **✓ 75/25 survival: cut marginal opens, avoid domination traps, take low-variance lines**
- 50/50: play your normal game, ignore the bubble
- 35/65: attack — they least expect it
- Fold everything except aces until the bubble bursts

> Covered on the bubble = 75/25 survival mode. Cut the marginal opens and dominated spots — but note the wrong answer isn't 'fold everything': blinding off has a cost too.

**Q9. Same bubble, but now you're the big stack covering a table of risk-averse mid-stacks. Your mode?**

- Stay tight — protect the lead you built
- **✓ Steal relentlessly and 3-bet blocker-heavy hands more often**
- Only play premiums; variance is the enemy
- Limp more to see cheap flops

> Covering on the bubble = 35/65 warlord mode. Their risk premium is your license to raid: relentless steals and blocker-heavy 3-bets against players who cannot fight back.

**Q10. At 15–25 big blinds, which hands gain value?**

- Small suited connectors — cheap flops, big paydays
- **✓ High-card and Broadway hands; suited connectors lose value**
- Small pairs — set mining is stack-depth-proof
- All hands equally; depth doesn't change values

> Short, the game turns preflop-centric: high-card and Broadway hands dominate jam/reshove ranges while speculative suited hands stop realizing their equity. Live players overplay 'pretty' hands here.

**Q11. Under 10 big blinds, the core discipline is…**

- Limp-trap your big hands to double up
- **✓ Jam-or-fold, preserving first-in fold equity — never bleed down from fear**
- Call more to see flops cheaply with the ante boost
- Wait for a premium regardless of blind pressure

> Jam-or-fold core. The fatal live leak is passivity — bleeding to 5bb without first-in aggression. Fold equity is the last canteen of water; don't let it evaporate.

**Q12. The bubble bursts and $15K min-cashes are locked. The field typically…**

- Tightens up — the money makes everyone careful
- Plays perfectly — relief clears the mind
- **✓ Relaxes incorrectly; you should re-accelerate and attack pay-jump psychology**
- Becomes unreadable chaos; sit tight

> Post-bubble is a raid window: 45/55, re-accelerate. Players lock into pay-jump psychology after cashing — attack the medium stacks that stop defending.

## 🧘 The Stillness (Zen Monk)

**Q13. Your nightly sleep target for all 100 days and the event itself:**

- 5–6 hours — grinders adapt
- 6–7 hours with weekend catch-up
- **✓ 7–9 hours, consistently**
- Whatever feels right; sleep is individual

> 7 to 9 hours, per the health baselines the plan is built on. In a multi-day marathon, sleep is a strategic weapon, not recovery admin.

**Q14. The weekly physical baseline in the program:**

- **✓ 150 minutes moderate activity plus two strength sessions**
- A daily 10K run — cardio is king
- Gym only on rest days from study
- None required; poker is mental

> 150 minutes of moderate activity plus two muscle-strengthening sessions — the adult baseline, matching how pros like Kornuth prepare. The gladiator maintains the body that carries the mind.

**Q15. Brutal bad beat, Level 9, cameras nearby. Your first move?**

- Fire back next hand — reassert dominance immediately
- **✓ Run your reset script: breathing pattern, posture, next-hand focus**
- Leave the table for 20 minutes to cool off
- Tighten up for the next hour to be safe

> The monk's answer: feel the emotion and still perform. A rehearsed reset script — breath, posture, next-hand cue — recovers you in minutes. Both 'fight back' and 'hide' are tilt in disguise.

**Q16. After every session, the thing you grade is…**

- Profit and loss — the only truth
- **✓ Decision quality: your A/B/C-game share, plus marked hands**
- How many pots you won
- Your table image at close

> Track decision quality, not outcomes. Self-grade A/B/C-game, mark hands, review the pattern. Results lie for weeks; process tells the truth daily.

**Q17. You sit down for Day 1, Level 1. Priority one, first orbit:**

- Raise your first playable hand to set an aggressive image
- **✓ Tag every player at the table by archetype**
- Play ultra-tight and just watch for an hour
- Chat everyone up to gather intel

> Tag every player in the first orbit — rec, reg, nit, aggro rec, unknown. Observation is the monk's opening move; the exploitation that follows is built on it.

**Q18. On tournament day, you wake…**

- 90 minutes before cards fly — maximize sleep
- **✓ 3.5 to 4.5 hours before start: hydrate, move, mental warm-up, arrive early**
- Whenever, as long as you make Level 1
- 5+ hours early for a long study session

> 3.5–4.5 hours before start. Hydration, light movement, a ten-minute mental warm-up, structure review, early arrival. The routine is the armor you put on before the arena.
